\title{Resource-NMCTS: Neural Monte Carlo Tree Search with Affine Boolean Preconditioning for Resource-Constrained Quantum Oracle Synthesis}
\author{Zixiang Zhou}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Quantum Boolean oracle synthesis is a central subroutine in many quantum
algorithms, yet direct algebraic-normal-form constructions can be expensive in
T-count, depth, and ancillary workspace.  We study oracle synthesis as a
logic-level search problem over compute/uncompute factorizations of Boolean
monomials.  The core \affinemethod{} solver combines resource-weighted neural
Monte Carlo tree search with affine Boolean input preconditioning, allowing the
search to change the XOR coordinate system before factoring the resulting
fixed-polarity Reed--Muller representation.  The full \method{} method adds a
resource-adaptive portfolio guard that selects, under the current weighted
objective, among direct ANF, fixed-coordinate MCTS, FPRM/affine neural MCTS, and
ESOP cube-beam candidates; it does not use SSHR candidates.  On the current
\dataset{} benchmark of 322 Boolean functions, \affinemethod{} is correct on
all functions and reduces mean T-count by 61.83\% relative to direct ANF
synthesis.  On a separate $n\leq 6$ traditional-baseline slice, \method{} has
172 T-count wins and no losses against ESOP cube beam search, and 162 T-count
wins, 1 loss, and 14 ties against time-limited weighted ESOP MILP.  A
resource-profile stress test shows that \method{} is best or tied on 42/47,
42/47, 40/47, and 42/47 functions under T-heavy, balanced, CNOT-depth-heavy, and
ancilla-tight objectives.  The method is evaluated only at the logical circuit
layer; hardware mapping and connectivity constraints are outside the present
scope.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Boolean oracles translate classical predicates into reversible quantum
subroutines.  In fault-tolerant settings, the cost of such oracles is often
dominated by non-Clifford resources, so synthesis methods must account for
T-count, depth, and ancillary workspace rather than only the number of reversible
gates.  Existing Boolean-oriented approaches include XAG-based compilation,
resource-constrained oracle synthesis, multiplicative-complexity methods, and
BDD-based reversible synthesis~\cite{meuli2022xag,meuli2020ros,meuli2019multiplicative,wille2009bdd}.

Recent learning-guided synthesis work has shown that reinforcement learning and
tree search can optimize quantum circuits in nontrivial spaces, including
Clifford+T unitary synthesis, non-Clifford-count optimization, MCTS-based
quantum circuit synthesis, and ZX-calculus rewriting~\cite{rietsch2024unitary,kremer2025nonclifford,machiya2026monteq,riu2025rlzx}.
However, these systems do not directly solve the symbolic Boolean oracle
problem studied here: choosing reusable Boolean intermediates under a
multi-resource objective.

This paper frames quantum Boolean oracle synthesis as a resource-constrained
search over algebraic-normal-form (ANF) and fixed-polarity Reed--Muller (FPRM)
factorizations.  The central claim is that affine changes of Boolean coordinates
can expose low-T decompositions that fixed-coordinate ANF factoring misses, and
that a resource-adaptive neural MCTS portfolio can retain these wins while
guarding against degradation under different weighted objectives.

\section{Problem Formulation}

Let $f:\{0,1\}^n\rightarrow\{0,1\}$ be a Boolean function.  The target oracle
maps
\[
  |x\rangle |y\rangle \mapsto |x\rangle |y \oplus f(x)\rangle .
\]
We represent $f$ by its ANF,
\[
  f(x) = \bigoplus_{m\in M}\prod_{i\in m} x_i,
\]
where each monomial is encoded as a bit mask.  Direct ANF synthesis applies one
controlled operation per monomial.  The search problem is to replace this direct
construction by reusable compute/uncompute subplans that lower the weighted
resource score
\[
  S = w_T T + w_C CNOT + w_D D + w_G G + w_A A,
\]
where $T$ is T-count, $D$ is logical depth, $G$ is gate count, and $A$ is peak
ancilla.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Resource-aware ANF factorization}

At each search state, the remaining ANF terms are a set of monomial masks.  An
action chooses a common factor $S$ appearing in at least two terms, computes
$S$ into a clean temporary ancilla, synthesizes the residual terms using that
ancilla as an additional control, uncomputes $S$, and then synthesizes the
remaining terms.  The implementation restricts the number of live factor
ancillae and ranks candidate factors by immediate weighted resource gain.

\subsection{Logical-AND cost model}

The reported resource objective uses a logical-AND accounting mode: temporary
ANDs are charged four T gates for compute and T-free Clifford/measurement-based
cleanup for uncompute.  The verifier circuit remains deterministic and is
checked by exhaustive classical simulation over the truth table.  Thus the
paper reports logic-level resource estimates rather than mapped hardware
circuits.

\subsection{Neural Monte Carlo tree search}

The MCTS solver recursively applies PUCT-style selection to factorization
actions.  Unvisited subproblems use a greedy rollout value.  A small neural
action scorer is trained from synthetic ANF states with rollout-improvement
labels, then used as an additive prior during action ranking.

\subsection{Affine Boolean preconditioning}

\affinemethod{} searches invertible linear transforms $y=Bx$ before ANF/FPRM
factorization.  For each candidate transform, it synthesizes
$h(y)=f(B^{-1}y)$ and wraps the body circuit by CNOT networks implementing and
undoing $B$.  Candidate transforms include the identity, star-like XOR
transforms, single-CNOT transforms, and random short CNOT sequences.  The
candidate list is first ranked by fast FPRM/greedy factoring; low-dimensional
instances are then refined by neural MCTS, while large instances use term-count
guards to avoid affine densification.

\subsection{Guard against fixed-coordinate degradation}

The affine plan is compared against a fixed-coordinate MCTS plan under the same
weighted score.  The lower-score plan is returned.  This guard makes the method
nondegrading in score on completed fixed-coordinate MCTS pairs, while preserving
affine improvements when coordinate changes reveal better factorizations.

\subsection{Resource-adaptive portfolio guard}

The full \method{} solver treats the weighted resource objective as a selection
criterion over internally generated candidates.  For each function it evaluates
direct ANF, fixed-coordinate logical-AND MCTS, FPRM/greedy factoring,
\affinemethod{}, and, for $n\leq 6$, ESOP cube-beam search.  It returns the
correct candidate with the smallest weighted score, breaking ties by T-count,
CNOT count, depth, and peak ancilla.  SSHR is used only as an external baseline
in the experiments and is not part of this portfolio.  This final guard converts
the affine neural search into a resource-constrained synthesis method: when
affine coordinates are best, they are retained; when a simpler internal
construction is better under the active weights, the method can select it.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Benchmark and baselines}

The current \dataset{} run contains 322 Boolean functions: structured oracles
such as parity, majority, threshold, mux, adders, and multipliers; random truth
tables for $n=4,5,6$; and random ANF functions for $n=8,10,12$.  We compare:
direct ANF, logical-AND direct ANF, fixed-coordinate logical-AND MCTS,
\affinemethod{}, and SSHR-H for functions with $n\leq 6$.
A separate \traditionaldataset{} slice restricts the benchmark to $n\leq 6$
and adds \method{}, ESOP cube beam search, and a time-limited weighted ESOP
MILP over candidate cubes.

To test whether the method remains competitive under different resource
objectives, we ran a 47-function $n\leq 6$ sweep under four profiles: T-heavy,
balanced, CNOT-depth-heavy, and ancilla-tight.  The sweep produced 1128
method/profile/function rows with no errors or skips.  Scores are comparable
within a profile but not across profiles because each profile uses a different
weighted objective.

\method{} was best or tied on 42/47 functions under T-heavy weights, 42/47

improves resource-objective robustness, but the search still needs more
profile-specific candidate generation before claiming a full Pareto optimizer.

\section{Discussion}

The results support the central hypothesis that affine Boolean coordinates are
a useful search dimension for quantum oracle synthesis and that a
resource-adaptive internal portfolio can remove avoidable score regressions.
The wins are strongest in T-count and composite resource score.  Against
SSHR-H, the method often uses more CNOTs and depth, which is expected because
SSHR-H is a CNOT-oriented small-function baseline and is not included in the
\method{} portfolio.  The present contribution should therefore be framed as
low-T, resource-constrained logical synthesis, not as a CNOT-only optimizer.

The runtime results show a practical tradeoff.  Affine-greedy captures most of
the T-count gain at very low runtime, making it the best fast setting in the
current implementation.  \method{} is slower than \affinemethod{} on the
$n\leq 6$ slice because it evaluates additional internal candidates, but its
median runtime remains below 1 s in the current small-function experiment.

The main current limitations are the absence of hardware mapping, the use of a
local benchmark suite rather than standardized external oracle benchmarks, and
the lack of a reproduced external XAG/ROS toolchain in the comparison set.  The
in-harness ESOP cube-beam and weighted-MILP baselines strengthen the Boolean
synthesis comparison, but external implementations remain necessary before a
submission-ready claim.  The resource-profile sweep also shows that the current
candidate generator is not yet strongly profile-sensitive; resource-specific
candidate generation is a natural next extension.

\section{Conclusion}

We introduced a logic-level quantum Boolean oracle synthesis method that
combines resource-aware neural MCTS, affine Boolean preconditioning, and a
resource-adaptive portfolio guard.  On the current benchmark, the affine neural
component substantially reduces T-count versus direct ANF, logical-AND direct
ANF, fixed-coordinate MCTS, and SSHR-H on supported functions.  On the
$n\leq 6$ traditional-baseline slice, the full \method{} method further avoids
score regressions against internal ESOP cube-beam candidates and remains
competitive against weighted ESOP MILP.  A resource-profile stress test shows
that this advantage is robust across several weighted objectives, while also
exposing limited profile sensitivity.  The next step is to add standardized
external baselines and stronger runtime-stress tests so the empirical claim is
submission-ready.
