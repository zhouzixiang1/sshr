\title{Resource-NMCTS: Neural Monte Carlo Tree Search with Affine Boolean Preconditioning for Resource-Constrained Quantum Oracle Synthesis}
\author{Zixiang Zhou}
\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
Quantum Boolean oracle synthesis is a central subroutine in many quantum
algorithms, yet direct algebraic-normal-form constructions can be expensive in
T-count, depth, and ancillary workspace.  We study oracle synthesis as a
logic-level search problem over compute/uncompute factorizations of Boolean
monomials.  The core \affinemethod{} solver combines resource-weighted neural
Monte Carlo tree search with affine Boolean input preconditioning, allowing the
search to change the XOR coordinate system before factoring the resulting
fixed-polarity Reed--Muller representation.  The full \method{} method adds a
budgeted resource-adaptive portfolio guard that selects, under the current
weighted objective, among direct ANF, FPRM/affine neural MCTS, and tractable
fixed-coordinate MCTS or ESOP cube-beam candidates; it does not use SSHR
candidates.  On the current
\dataset{} benchmark of 322 Boolean functions, \affinemethod{} is correct on
all functions and reduces mean T-count by 61.83\% relative to direct ANF
synthesis.  On a separate $n\leq 6$ traditional-baseline slice, the
\paretomethod{} archive has the lowest mean T-count and weighted score, and
improves on \method{} with 74 score wins, no losses, and 103 ties.  It has
174 score wins and no losses against ESOP cube beam search, and 167 score
wins, 3 losses, and 7 ties against time-limited weighted ESOP MILP.  On an
exported external comparison, \method{} has 69 score wins and 3 losses against
the exact $n\leq 4$ CNOT-optimized SSHR-I pilot, 168 score wins and 9
losses against the time-limited $n\leq 6$ SSHR-I extension, and 177 score wins
with no losses against an ABC-AIG baseline on the same $n\leq 6$ export.  It
also has 177 score wins with no losses against ABC-XAG/GIA, ABC-LUT, and
BDD/Shannon baselines, and 147 score wins, 24 losses, and 6 ties against ABC's
quantum-cost ESOP minimization path.  A
resource-profile stress test shows that \paretomethod{} is best or tied on
44/47, 44/47, 42/47, and 43/47 functions under T-heavy, balanced,
CNOT-depth-heavy, and ancilla-tight objectives, improving on
\profilemethod{} with no score losses in any profile.  A larger core benchmark
through $n=12$ contains 330 functions and 1980 method/function rows; \method{}
and \profilemethod{} complete all functions, and \method{} reduces mean
T-count by 60.37\% relative to direct ANF and by 37.25\% relative to
logical-AND direct ANF, while fixed-coordinate MCTS times out on five $n=12$
rows.  An isolated
$n=14$ stress benchmark adds 64 random ANF functions and 512 rows with no
errors or skips; the guarded high-dimensional variants reduce mean T-count by
57.42\% relative to direct ANF and by 32.74\% relative to logical-AND direct
ANF, with 60 score wins, no losses, and 4 ties against FPRM-greedy.  A
follow-up $n=15$ scale check preserves this pattern with 30 score wins, no
losses, and 2 ties against FPRM-greedy, reducing mean weighted score by
5.73\%.  A further $n=16$ scale check contains 24 random ANF functions and 144
rows with no errors or skips; the ultra-scale guard matches its one-layer
linear-pair child, reduces mean T-count by 62.08\% relative to direct ANF, and
has 22 score wins, no losses, and 2 ties against bounded FPRM root-beam.  On
exported high-dimensional ABC AIG/XAG/LUT and BDD checks for the same $n=14$
and $n=15$ sets, the guarded method wins all 384 weighted-score
comparisons with 93.24\%--97.76\% mean score reductions.  ABC-AIG and ABC-XAG
keep lower estimated depth on most functions, while ABC-LUT and BDD are deeper
under our sequential network estimates.  The method is
evaluated only at the logical circuit layer;
hardware mapping and connectivity constraints are outside the present scope.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Boolean oracles translate classical predicates into reversible quantum
subroutines.  In fault-tolerant settings, the cost of such oracles is often
dominated by non-Clifford resources, so synthesis methods must account for
T-count, depth, and ancillary workspace rather than only the number of reversible
gates.  Existing Boolean-oriented approaches provide strong representations and
baseline synthesis methods, while recent learning-guided circuit work shows
that neural policies and tree search can be useful in large synthesis spaces.
The gap addressed here is the combination of these two lines: a neural search
method whose state and actions remain symbolic Boolean oracle constructions and
whose objective directly scores T-count, CNOT count, depth, gates, and ancilla.

This paper frames quantum Boolean oracle synthesis as a resource-constrained
search over algebraic-normal-form (ANF) and fixed-polarity Reed--Muller (FPRM)
factorizations.  The central claim is that affine changes of Boolean coordinates
can expose low-T decompositions that fixed-coordinate ANF factoring misses, and
that a resource-adaptive neural MCTS portfolio can retain these wins while
guarding against degradation under different weighted objectives.

\section{Related Work and Gap}

Boolean-structure methods are the closest technical baselines.  XAG-based
quantum compilation uses XOR-and-AND logic networks to reduce fault-tolerant
resources; ROS combines LUT mapping with resource-aware oracle synthesis and
SAT-based garbage management; multiplicative-complexity methods relate the
number of AND nodes in a Boolean network to T-count and ancilla upper bounds;
ABC provides a mature AIG rewriting and verification baseline for Boolean logic;
and BDD-based reversible synthesis scales to large Boolean functions by
compiling decision-diagram
structure~\cite{meuli2022xag,meuli2020ros,meuli2019multiplicative,brayton2010abc,wille2009bdd}.
SSHR is also closely related because it exploits parallelotope structure in the
Boolean hypercube and provides CNOT-oriented small-function baselines,
including heuristic and ILP variants~\cite{zheng2025sshr}.  These methods
motivate the resource metrics and symbolic action space used here, but they do
not use a learned policy/value search over ANF/FPRM factorization plans.

Learning-guided synthesis has developed along adjacent axes.  ShortCircuit uses
an AlphaZero-style process for classical AIG generation from truth tables,
showing that policy/value search can help Boolean circuit construction at the
logic level~\cite{tsaras2024shortcircuit}.  Quantum learning-guided work has
used Gumbel AlphaZero for Clifford+T unitary synthesis, reinforcement learning
for non-Clifford-count optimization, MCTS for Hamiltonian-simulation Pauli-term
ordering, and PPO/GNN policies for ZX-calculus rewriting~\cite{rietsch2024unitary,kremer2025nonclifford,machiya2026monteq,riu2025rlzx}.
These systems validate the use of neural search in synthesis, but they do not
directly target Boolean oracle factorization with explicit T/CNOT/depth/ancilla
tradeoffs.

The present method therefore avoids two weak directions for this problem: it is
not a gate-by-gate unitary generator, and it is not an SSHR parallelotope
selector.  It searches over reusable Boolean intermediates in ANF/FPRM form,
uses affine coordinate changes as a cheap way to alter the factorization basis,
and treats external XAG, ROS, BDD, ESOP, and SSHR-style flows as baselines or
export targets rather than as components of the method.

\section{Problem Formulation}

Let $f:\{0,1\}^n\rightarrow\{0,1\}$ be a Boolean function.  The target oracle
maps
\[
  |x\rangle |y\rangle \mapsto |x\rangle |y \oplus f(x)\rangle .
\]
We represent $f$ by its ANF,
\[
  f(x) = \bigoplus_{m\in M}\prod_{i\in m} x_i,
\]
where each monomial is encoded as a bit mask.  Direct ANF synthesis applies one
controlled operation per monomial.  The search problem is to replace this direct
construction by reusable compute/uncompute subplans that lower the weighted
resource score
\[
  S = w_T T + w_C CNOT + w_D D + w_G G + w_A A,
\]
where $T$ is T-count, $D$ is logical depth, $G$ is gate count, and $A$ is peak
ancilla.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Resource-aware ANF factorization}

At each search state, the remaining ANF terms are a set of monomial masks.  An
action chooses a common factor $S$ appearing in at least two terms, computes
$S$ into a clean temporary ancilla, synthesizes the residual terms using that
ancilla as an additional control, uncomputes $S$, and then synthesizes the
remaining terms.  The implementation restricts the number of live factor
ancillae and ranks candidate factors by immediate weighted resource gain.

\subsection{Logical-AND cost model}

The reported resource objective uses a logical-AND accounting mode: temporary
ANDs are charged four T gates for compute and T-free Clifford/measurement-based
cleanup for uncompute.  The verifier circuit remains deterministic and is
checked by exhaustive classical simulation over the truth table.  Thus the
paper reports logic-level resource estimates rather than mapped hardware
circuits.

\subsection{Neural Monte Carlo tree search}

The MCTS solver recursively applies PUCT-style selection to factorization
actions.  Unvisited subproblems use a greedy rollout value.  A small neural
action scorer is trained from synthetic ANF states with rollout-improvement
labels, then used as an additive prior during action ranking.

\subsection{Affine Boolean preconditioning}

\affinemethod{} searches invertible linear transforms $y=Bx$ before ANF/FPRM
factorization.  For each candidate transform, it synthesizes
$h(y)=f(B^{-1}y)$ and wraps the body circuit by CNOT networks implementing and
undoing $B$.  Candidate transforms include the identity, star-like XOR
transforms, single-CNOT transforms, and random short CNOT sequences.  The
candidate list is first ranked by fast FPRM/greedy factoring; low-dimensional
instances are then refined by neural MCTS, while large instances use term-count
guards to avoid affine densification.

\subsection{Guard against fixed-coordinate degradation}

The affine plan is compared against a fixed-coordinate MCTS plan under the same
weighted score.  The lower-score plan is returned.  This guard makes the method
nondegrading in score on completed fixed-coordinate MCTS pairs, while preserving
affine improvements when coordinate changes reveal better factorizations.

\subsection{Resource-adaptive portfolio guard}

The full \method{} solver treats the weighted resource objective as a selection
criterion over internally generated candidates.  For each function it evaluates
direct ANF, FPRM/greedy factoring, and a budgeted \affinemethod{} candidate.
When the configured dimension guard permits it, the portfolio also evaluates
fixed-coordinate logical-AND MCTS; for $n\leq 6$, it additionally evaluates
ESOP cube-beam search.  For $n>12$, where full fixed-coordinate MCTS and broad
affine search create long tails, it replaces the expensive branches by a
bounded recursive FPRM linear-pair candidate: many polarities are screened by
direct cost, a root-child beam baseline is retained, and repeated term pairs
$x_i m \oplus x_j m$ can be factored as $(x_i \oplus x_j)m$ using a temporary
CNOT-computed linear control.  One extra recursive layer applies the same pair
search to both the quotient and the remaining terms when the subproblem has at
most 900 terms.
It returns the correct candidate with the smallest
weighted score, breaking ties by T-count, CNOT count, depth, and peak ancilla.
SSHR is used only as an external baseline in the experiments and is not part of
this portfolio.  This final guard converts the affine neural search into a
resource-constrained synthesis method: when affine coordinates are best, they
are retained; when a simpler internal construction is better under the active
weights, the method can select it.  The guard is budgeted rather than an
all-scale dominance certificate: high-dimensional runs deliberately avoid
internal candidates that create long runtime tails.

\subsection{Profile-aware candidate generation}

\profilemethod{} adds a second portfolio layer for explicit resource profiles.
For $n\leq 6$, it evaluates additional T-aggressive, CNOT/depth-oriented, and
ancilla-tight FPRM, affine-greedy, and ESOP cube-beam candidates before
selecting by the active weighted score.  For larger random-ANF instances, the
same method delegates to the high-dimensional Resource-NMCTS guard: it keeps
direct ANF and the bounded recursive FPRM linear-pair candidate, but avoids
expensive fixed-coordinate MCTS guards.  A width-three linear-parity factor is
reported as a standalone ablation in the $n=15$ scaling check, but the
portfolio uses the recursive pair candidate because it dominates the parity
ablation under the weighted objective.
This design separates two roles.  On small functions it tests whether the
resource profile can change the selected circuit; on large functions it acts as
a runtime-tail guard rather than a score-dominance guarantee.

\subsection{Pareto resource archive}

\paretomethod{} strengthens the small-function resource-profile search by
changing the internal objective used to generate candidates, not only the
search budget.  For each function it evaluates child searches under five
candidate-generation objectives: the active score, a T-sparse score, a
CNOT/depth-heavy score, an ancilla-tight score, and a gate-count-sensitive
score.  The resulting candidates include FPRM greedy, bounded root-child beam,
CNOT-only linear-pair factoring, affine-greedy, selected affine-neural, and
ESOP cube-beam plans when the dimension guard permits them.  The method then
removes candidates dominated simultaneously in T-count, CNOT count, depth,
gate count, and peak ancilla, and finally selects from this non-dominated
archive using the active resource profile.  Thus the method keeps the same
logical verifier and cost model as \method{}, but explicitly approximates a
local Pareto frontier before profile-specific selection.
On $n\leq 6$ functions, the archive also includes a ranked polarity candidate:
all $2^n$ FPRM polarities are screened under greedy, root-child-beam, and
CNOT-only linear-pair factorizations, and the best scored plan is added to the
non-dominated pool.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Benchmark and baselines}

The current \dataset{} run contains 322 Boolean functions: structured oracles
such as parity, majority, threshold, mux, adders, and multipliers; random truth
tables for $n=4,5,6$; and random ANF functions for $n=8,10,12$.  We compare:
direct ANF, logical-AND direct ANF, fixed-coordinate logical-AND MCTS,
\affinemethod{}, and SSHR-H for functions with $n\leq 6$.
A separate \traditionaldataset{} slice restricts the benchmark to $n\leq 6$
and adds \method{}, ESOP cube beam search, and a time-limited weighted ESOP
MILP over candidate cubes.  The \largedataset{} benchmark extends the same
resource objective to a larger random-ANF core through $n=12$.  To support
external reproduction, the benchmark generator can export every preset to PLA,
BLIF \texttt{.names}, and truth-table JSON manifests.  The current draft adds
an exported exact-SSHR-I pilot on the $n\leq 4$ subset, a time-limited
$n\leq 6$ SSHR-I extension, Berkeley ABC AIG, XAG/GIA, LUT, and ESOP
baselines, and a reduced ordered BDD baseline covering the full
\traditionaldataset{} slice.  The ABC-AIG, ABC-XAG, ABC-LUT, and BDD export
paths are also run on the isolated $n=14$ and $n=15$ high-dimensional
random-ANF suites; reproduced ROS/mockturtle reversible-toolchain results are
still future work.

\subsection{Exported external baselines}

To check the external-tool exchange path, we exported the
\traditionaldataset{} benchmark to PLA, BLIF, and truth-table JSON, then ran
separate external backends over the exported manifest.  The SSHR backend gives
CNOT-oriented heuristic and ILP baselines.  The ABC-AIG backend optimizes the
exported BLIF with ABC rewriting, verifies the optimized BLIF truth table, and
maps AIG AND count and level to a logic-level Bennett compute/uncompute
resource estimate.  The ABC-XAG backend uses ABC's GIA \texttt{\&st -m -L 1}
path, records XOR/MUX/AND statistics with \texttt{\&ps -m -x}, verifies the
emitted BLIF truth table, and scores the network with a Bennett-style logical
XAG estimate.  The ABC-ESOP backend runs ABC's quantum-cost
\texttt{\&exorcism -q} flow, verifies the emitted ESOP-PLA against the exported
truth table, and scores the resulting XOR-of-cubes oracle using the same
logical-AND cube model as the internal ESOP baselines.  The ABC-LUT backend maps
the same BLIF through \texttt{if -K 4}, verifies the mapped BLIF, and scores a
compute/output/uncompute network whose local LUT functions are converted to
ANF terms.  The BDD backend builds reduced ordered BDDs under several
deterministic variable orders, verifies the decision diagram against the
exported truth table, and scores a
compute/uncompute Shannon network.  For the 72 functions
with $n\leq 4$, the SSHR-I runs are
exact within the configured Gurobi solve.  This exact pilot produced 216
external rows with no errors or skips and confirmed that \method{} retains a
weighted-score advantage against both CNOT-optimized and T-optimized SSHR-I,
while spending more CNOTs.

The exact $n\leq 4$ pilot strengthens but also sharpens the claim.  Against
CNOT-optimized SSHR-I, \method{} has 65 T-count wins, no T-count losses, and 7
ties; by weighted score it has 69 wins, 3 losses, and no ties, with a 26.45\%

We then ran the external extension over all 177 exported functions with
$n\leq 6$.  This produced 1416 external rows with no errors or skips: 531 SSHR
rows, 177 ABC-AIG rows, 177 ABC-XAG rows, 177 ABC-LUT rows, 177 BDD rows, and
177 ABC-ESOP rows.
Against the CNOT-optimized SSHR-I extension, \method{} had 164 T-count wins, 3
losses, and 10 ties, and 168 score wins with
9 score losses; the mean score reduction was 27.92\%.  Against the T-optimized
extension, \method{} had 166 T-count wins, 1 loss, and 10 ties, with a 26.25\%
mean score reduction.  The CNOT disadvantage persisted, with \method{} worse on
168/177 CNOT comparisons against the CNOT-optimized run.

The ABC-AIG comparison strengthens the resource-constrained claim from a
different direction.  \method{} had 170 T-count wins, 2 losses, and 5 ties
against ABC-AIG; it won all 177 CNOT, peak-ancilla, and weighted-score
comparisons, reducing mean weighted score by 54.52\% and mean peak ancilla by
86.29\%.  ABC-AIG had a lower depth estimate on 126/177 functions, which is
consistent with a shallow optimized AIG followed by a wide Bennett embedding.
This makes ABC-AIG a useful foil for the ancilla and weighted-resource
objective, not a claim that \method{} dominates every metric.

The ABC-XAG/GIA comparison adds an XOR-aware network baseline without using an
external mapper inside the method.  \method{} had 176 T-count wins, no losses,
and 1 tie against ABC-XAG; it won all 177 peak-ancilla and weighted-score
comparisons, reducing mean weighted score by 63.23\% and mean peak ancilla by
89.85\%.  It also reduced mean CNOT by 35.53\%, although ABC-XAG retained a
lower depth estimate on 144/177 functions.  This closes part of the earlier
XAG comparison gap while still falling short of a full ROS or mockturtle
reversible-toolchain reproduction.

The ABC-LUT comparison adds a mapped-LUT network baseline closer to ROS/LUT
families, though still without a full reversible LUT mapper.  \method{} won all
177 CNOT, depth, peak-ancilla, and weighted-score comparisons against ABC-LUT,
with 175 T-count wins and 2 ties.  The mean weighted-score reduction was
76.41\%.  This result shows that a naive compute/uncompute embedding of
LUT-mapped BLIF is not competitive under the current logical-resource model,
but it narrows the external LUT comparison gap.

The BDD comparison adds a scalable decision-diagram baseline.  \method{} won
all 177 T-count, CNOT, depth, peak-ancilla, and weighted-score comparisons
against BDD, reducing mean weighted score by 67.15\%.  This result should be
read as a comparison to a verified logic-level Shannon-network embedding of
ROBDDs rather than to every optimized BDD-based reversible synthesis variant.

The ABC-ESOP comparison is closer to the XOR-of-products synthesis family.
\method{} had 144 T-count wins, 19 losses, and 14 ties against ABC-ESOP, and
147 score wins, 24 losses, and 6 ties.  The mean weighted-score reduction was
19.88\%; unlike the SSHR-I CNOT-oriented comparison, \method{} also had a small
mean CNOT reduction of 1.19\%.  This result is not a substitute for a reproduced
ROS mapper, but it adds a standard external XOR/ESOP minimizer to the
comparison boundary.

Finally, we pushed the ABC AIG/XAG/LUT and BDD exchange beyond the small-function
slice.  The \highdimdataset{} and \scaledataset{} suites were exported to
BLIF/truth JSON, optimized or represented by the external-style baselines, and
verified by a bit-parallel full truth-table checker before scoring.  The
high-dimensional rows contain 64 $n=14$ and 32 $n=15$ functions for each of
ABC-AIG, ABC-XAG, ABC-LUT, and BDD, all correct and with no errors or skips.
Against all four baselines, \method{} and \profilemethod{} win every T-count,
CNOT, peak-ancilla, and weighted-score comparison on these 384 external
comparisons; the mean score reductions range from 93.24\% to 97.76\%.  The
depth column
remains an important counterweight for ABC: ABC-AIG has a lower estimated depth
on 50/64 and 25/32 high-dimensional functions, and ABC-XAG has a lower
estimated depth on 50/64 and 26/32 functions, respectively.  ABC-LUT and BDD
depths are much higher under the sequential network estimates.  Thus the
high-dimensional external result supports the low-ancilla and weighted-resource
claim, but not depth-only dominance against optimized ABC networks.

\subsection{Runtime and resource tradeoff}

The ablation also exposes the computational cost of the search components.
Affine-greedy completed all 322 functions with a 0.03 s median runtime and a

\section{Discussion}

The results support the central hypothesis that affine Boolean coordinates are
a useful search dimension for quantum oracle synthesis and that a
resource-adaptive internal portfolio can reduce avoidable score regressions
under explicit budget guards.
The wins are strongest in T-count and composite resource score.  Against
SSHR-H, the method often uses more CNOTs and depth, which is expected because
SSHR-H is a CNOT-oriented small-function baseline and is not included in the
\method{} portfolio.  The present contribution should therefore be framed as
low-T, resource-constrained logical synthesis, not as a CNOT-only optimizer.

The runtime results show a practical tradeoff.  Affine-greedy captures most of
the T-count gain at very low runtime, making it the best fast setting in the
current implementation.  \method{} is slower than \affinemethod{} on the
$n\leq 6$ slice because it evaluates additional internal candidates, but its
median runtime remains below 1 s in the current small-function experiment.  On
the \largedataset{} benchmark, \method{} completes every function with median
runtime 1.31 s and p95 runtime 58.86 s; the fixed-coordinate MCTS baseline has
five timeouts.  The guarded \profilemethod{} variant reduces the p95 runtime to
25.68 s on the same large benchmark, at the cost of a higher mean score.  On
the isolated $n=14$ stress test, both guarded variants complete all 64
functions and improve over FPRM-greedy with no score losses; the $n=16$ scale
check also completes without errors after switching to the ultra-scale
one-layer linear-pair guard.  This shows that
the current high-dimensional guard is no longer only a scalability fallback:
pairwise XOR factoring can correct a measurable part of the greedy
Reed--Muller search error using only Clifford linear preprocessing around the
factored subplan.

The main current limitations are the absence of hardware mapping, the fact that
the exported SSHR-I extension beyond $n\leq 4$ is time-limited rather than an
exact certificate, the fact that ABC/BDD/ESOP/LUT rows are scored through
logic-level estimates rather than full reversible-toolchain synthesis, and the
lack of a reproduced external ROS/mockturtle toolchain in the comparison set.
At $n=16$, the guarded Resource/Profile variants tie their own one-layer
linear-pair child, so the ultra result is bounded scalability evidence rather
than a new search-quality separation.
The in-harness ESOP cube-beam and weighted-MILP baselines plus the exported
SSHR-I/ABC/BDD/LUT comparisons strengthen the Boolean synthesis comparison, and
the new high-dimensional ABC/BDD/LUT rows show that this external comparison is no
longer restricted to $n\leq 6$.  More external implementations remain necessary
before a submission-ready claim.
The resource-profile sweep shows that objective-specific candidate generation
and non-dominated archive selection improve small-function profile adaptation.
The large benchmark still shows the corresponding high-dimensional tradeoff:
conservative profile guards reduce runtime tails but give up some score.  The
$n=14$ stress test narrows this limitation further: bounded recursive FPRM
linear-pair search keeps the run finite and improves over FPRM-greedy on most
functions, and the $n=15$ scale check preserves that margin while keeping all
runs finite.  The remaining weakness is less the internal high-dimensional
guard than the comparison boundary: the current evidence still lacks
reproduced ROS or mockturtle reversible-toolchain
baselines.

\section{Conclusion}

We introduced a logic-level quantum Boolean oracle synthesis method that
combines resource-aware neural MCTS, affine Boolean preconditioning, and a
resource-adaptive portfolio guard.  On the current benchmark, the affine neural
component substantially reduces T-count versus direct ANF, logical-AND direct
ANF, fixed-coordinate MCTS, and SSHR-H on supported functions.  On the
$n\leq 6$ traditional-baseline slice, \paretomethod{} further improves the
balanced weighted score over \method{}, dominates internal ESOP cube-beam
candidates by weighted score, and remains competitive against weighted ESOP
MILP.  Resource-profile and \largedataset{} benchmarks show that this
advantage is robust across several weighted objectives and larger random ANF
instances.  Exported SSHR, ABC-AIG, ABC-XAG, ABC-LUT, BDD, and ABC-ESOP
baselines add independent cross-tool evidence that the gains
are not an artifact of the internal experiment harness, while also showing that
CNOT and depth remain important limitations.  The new high-dimensional stress
tests show that bounded guards scale without timeouts, beat FPRM-greedy on
60 of 64 weighted-score comparisons at $n=14$ and 30 of 32 comparisons at
$n=15$, complete all 144 $n=16$ ultra-scale rows under the one-layer guard
while retaining score wins against FPRM root-beam, and beat exported ABC
AIG/XAG/LUT and BDD on all 384 high-dimensional weighted-score comparisons.
The next step is to reproduce ROS/mockturtle-style
toolchain baselines and hardware-aware mappings so the empirical claim is
submission-ready.
